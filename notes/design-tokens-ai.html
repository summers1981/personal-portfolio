Design tokens are the wrong abstraction for AI — and what to use instead

AI March 2026 · 3 min read

Design tokens solved a real problem. They gave design and engineering teams a shared vocabulary — a way to encode decisions about colour, spacing, and typography into a form that could move between tools without losing meaning. For human-to-human communication, they work well.

But when an AI encounters a token like color.brand.primary, something breaks down. The name signals structure — a namespace, a hierarchy — but it doesn't signal intent. The model has no grip on what "brand primary" means in context. Is this a background? An interactive element? A state? A constraint? Without that context, the model has to guess.

"Token names tell a machine what a decision is called. They rarely tell it why the decision was made — and that's the part AI actually needs."

The problem with abstraction layers

Tokens are an abstraction layer built for design-to-code pipelines. They assume a human in the middle — someone who can look up what color.brand.primary resolves to, understand the brand context, and apply it appropriately. Remove that human, and the abstraction stops being useful. It becomes noise.

LLMs reason by pattern-matching across context. They're good at inferring intent from well-structured, semantically rich inputs. A token name like color.interactive.default gives more to work with than color.brand.primary — but it still leaves the model guessing about scope, state, and relationship to other tokens.

What LLMs actually need

Through hands-on work structuring design systems for AI use, I've found three things make the difference:

  • Purpose-first naming. Tokens should express what they do, not what they belong to. color.action.primary.default over color.brand.blue. The semantic layer needs to be in the name itself, not in a doc a model will never see.
  • Structured usage constraints. A token needs to know — and communicate — where it can and can't be used. This isn't prose documentation. It's structured metadata: allowed contexts, forbidden contexts, relationships to other tokens.
  • Intent anchors. Short, structured descriptions of what a decision is trying to achieve. Not "this is the primary brand colour" but "use for the single most important interactive element in a view — buttons, primary CTAs only."

A different contract

The shift I'm advocating for isn't a renaming exercise. It's a rethinking of what a token is for. Tokens were built to communicate a decision to a pipeline. AI-ready tokens need to communicate a decision to a reasoner — something that will apply the token in novel contexts the original author never anticipated.

That means tokens need more metadata, not less. They need to be opinionated about their own use. And they need to be written with the assumption that the person applying them might be a model that has never seen your product, your brand guidelines, or your design history.

"The best AI-ready token is one that carries its own usage rules — so the model doesn't have to infer them."

What this looks like in practice

In practice, this means treating token documentation as structured data rather than prose commentary. Each token gets a machine-parseable block: name, purpose, allowed contexts, related tokens, visual output, and deprecation status.

It also means revisiting the token taxonomy itself. Rather than organising by origin (brand, product, system), organise by function: action tokens, feedback tokens, layout tokens, content tokens. Functions are what AI tools reason about. Origins are for humans who already know the brand.

The result is a design system that's still readable by designers and engineers — but that also works as a reliable knowledge base for AI-assisted product building. The abstraction layer doesn't disappear; it gets richer, more intentional, and more useful to everyone.
